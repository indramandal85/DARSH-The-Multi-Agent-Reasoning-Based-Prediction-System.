India Faces Oil Price Surge and Western Pressure Over Ukraine: March 2022

India found itself in a difficult geopolitical position in early March 2022 as oil prices surged past 100 dollars per barrel following Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Western allies including the United States and European Union urged India to condemn Russia and join sanctions, while India maintained its traditionally neutral stance.

Crude oil above 100 dollars threatened to significantly worsen India's import bill and fuel domestic inflation already running above comfortable levels. The rupee came under pressure as the current account deficit was expected to widen. The Reserve Bank of India was watching the situation closely.

Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman acknowledged the challenges from high global commodity prices but said India would manage through fiscal measures and diplomacy. She noted that India could not afford to alienate either Russia, which supplies defense equipment, or Western nations that are important trade and investment partners.

The government quietly continued to purchase Russian oil at discounted prices, drawing criticism from the US State Department. India's foreign ministry said the country would continue to act in its national interest and that Europe itself was still buying Russian energy.

Market participants remained cautious rather than panicking. Analysts noted that India had foreign exchange reserves of over 600 billion dollars that provided significant cushion. The diplomatic balancing act was seen as difficult but manageable.

Economists warned that sustained oil above 100 dollars would require India to either raise fuel prices significantly, which would hurt consumers and fuel inflation, or absorb the cost through fiscal subsidies that would strain the budget. The government chose a middle path, making modest fuel price adjustments while keeping larger increases in check.
