India Demonetization Shock: November 2016

Prime Minister Narendra Modi announced on November 8, 2016 that Rs 500 and Rs 1000 currency notes would cease to be legal tender from midnight, giving citizens only 50 days to exchange old notes at banks. The move demonetized approximately 86 percent of India's currency in circulation overnight.

The announcement sent shockwaves through the Indian economy. Long queues formed outside banks and ATMs across the country as citizens rushed to exchange their cash. Rural areas and small businesses were particularly hard hit as the informal economy, which runs almost entirely on cash, came to a near standstill.

The Reserve Bank of India and State Bank of India struggled to manage the massive demand for new currency notes. Hospitals reported patients being turned away, wedding ceremonies were disrupted, and daily wage workers lost income as employers could not pay in valid currency.

Economists from IIM Ahmedabad and other institutions warned that the short-term disruption would severely impact GDP growth. The agricultural sector, which relies heavily on cash transactions during the sowing season, faced acute distress.

The government defended the move as necessary to eliminate black money, counterfeit currency, and terror financing. Finance Minister Arun Jaitley assured citizens the disruption would be temporary and the long-term benefits would outweigh the short-term pain.

Opposition parties led by the Congress and Aam Aadmi Party called for nationwide protests, with Rahul Gandhi and Arvind Kejriwal calling the move an economic disaster that hurt ordinary citizens while failing to target actual black money holders.
